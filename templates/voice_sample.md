"The eleventh take of the afternoon put me at 9 total jumps across two moving vehicles, which was 5 more than any sane insurance policy would cover not to mention my left knee was about ready to explode with rage.
I stood on the running board of the pursuit car — a blacked-out Escalade doing forty-three miles per hour down a closed stretch of highway outside Palmdale — and watched the camera drone dip too low again. Right on cue, a voice shot out through my earpiece. Cut. Cut cut cut.

Garrett Foles's voice crackled over the radio. It bounced inside of my skull and rattled around mixed in with the accumulated heat and exhaust fumes of the day. The light was going to start waning and Garret’s voice took on a grating, fingernail on chalkboard quality now. I’ve worked with scores of directors. They all have their idiosyncrasies, quirks and personality disorders that I eventually came to appreciate. Today, I don’t know, it was different, and the name Foles just made me want to shout Goddam in the most unprofessional way.

Garrett Foles. Son of Richard Foles. Grandson of Walter Foles. Hollywood legends. Well, Richard and Walter anyway. Garret had directed exactly one feature film prior to this one, a ninety-four-minute adaptation of a short story his father had optioned, starring his college roommate and his college roommate's actual girlfriend, which had premiered at Sundance in an obscure slot and had been described by one reviewer as "competently assembled." A man who wore a bucket hat unironically and at this very moment, was probably standing behind a monitor telling someone that the light wasn't quite right. Nepo baby, that’s what they call them. I could think of 50 directors more qualified and deserving of this job. Oh well.

The light was fine. The light had been fine on take two. The problem on take two was that Garrett had wanted the camera angle lower, and the problem on takes three and four was that the camera angle was now too low, and the problem I kept entirely to myself was that there would come a point where my body would stop absorbing the cumulative impact of throwing itself at a moving SUV and just might end badly. Not that anyone gives a damn about me.
I accepted a water bottle from the PA and took stock of the situation as I jumped off the running board.
The car coordinator and driver of the SUV, a sun-damaged veteran named Polk who had been doing this since before I had my driver's license, leaned through the window with a weary apologetic expression that said: I know. I also know we're going again. I'm sorry about your knee. We had worked together on four productions and there was a silent understanding between us that this wasn’t great. 

I nodded. My knee throbbed. At least this is going to be a good payday.
